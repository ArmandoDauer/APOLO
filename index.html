APOLO

Demand-responsive transport (DRT) refers to a broad category of transport systems that operate as an intermediate mode between traditional public transport (PT) and private services such as taxis. These systems incorporate the on-demand flexibility of taxis—allowing pick-up locations, destinations, and schedules to be partially or fully adjusted to meet passenger needs—while retaining the ride-sharing characteristics of conventional PT.

DRT has been around for over 40 years and has consistently proven to be a viable alternative to fixed-route public transport, particularly in areas where demand is low, dispersed, or highly variable. The most important characteristic of DRT is its quick adaptability to the demand. This is achieved by flexibility on routes, fleet deployed, pick-up and drop-off locations, and/or schedule, enabled by a wide range of possible operation configurations.

However, this same flexibility introduces considerable complexity to the system design process, which can be especially challenging for stakeholders with limited expertise in the field. Poorly designed systems risk being misaligned with user needs, leading to inefficiencies and limited adoption.

While several methodologies for DRT planning exist in the academic literature, most focus on specific elements of the design process and fail to offer a comprehensive framework. There remains a lack of an integrated approach that simultaneously considers the service’s strategic objectives, characteristics of the service area and target users, the required level of flexibility, and operational constraints. This gap underscores the need for a holistic design methodology to support the effective implementation of DRT systems.

In response, this study proposes APOLO, a structured framework developed to support the design of efficient and context-appropriate DRT solutions, particularly in low-density areas. APOLO guides decision-makers through an iterative process that integrates scenario analysis—considering factors such as the purpose of implementation, service area, target users, and operating hours—with insights drawn from real-world DRT deployments. The framework aims to facilitate the selection of the most suitable DRT configuration to meet both operational and user-oriented goals.
