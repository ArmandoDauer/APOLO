Objectives Reached?

For a transportation system to be efficient and effective in achieving its intended objectives, it must be well-adapted to the socio-political and economic context of the service area in which it operates. In the case of DRT systems, these regional characteristics influence, among other aspects, the system’s degree of flexibility.

Highly flexible systems may be less appealing to users with time-sensitive needs, while systems with low flexibility may prompt potential passengers to opt for private vehicles. As such, flexibility is one of the key characteristics influencing passengers’ perceptions of a system’s usefulness—particularly regarding how changes in flexibility affect their willingness to use the service.

To incorporate this aspect, MUSE includes the impact of flexibility in the scenario under analysis through a set of input elements that reflect passenger perceptions on the topic. This perception is captured using a matrix that evaluates the flexibility associated with each operational variable, along with how flexible each of its possible values is.

  • Effect: For each variable, the decision-maker must determine whether an increase in flexibility positively or negatively affects passengers’ perception of the system’s utility (i.e., whether more flexibility in that variable better meets their transport needs).
  • Intensity: On a five-point scale ranging from “Weak” to “Strong,” decision-makers evaluate how impactful an increase in flexibility for a given variable would be on users’ decisions to use or avoid the service.
  • Flexibility of Value: Each value of the operational variables is classified based on how flexible it is, using a five-level scale from “No flexibility” to “Highly flexible.”

Pick-UP and Drop-off Locations

Classification of Pick-up and Drop-off possible values in terms of flexibility

Specific Request Point: Passengers are picked up and dropped off precisely at their origin and destination points. Among the possible values for this variable, it offers the highest level of flexibility. This type of journey is highly customizable, enhancing passenger comfort and potentially improving system accessibility for certain population groups. However, it also presents challenges in terms of planning efficiency and may increase travel time and distance for other trip requests within the system.

Close to the Request Point: Passengers are picked up and dropped off near their trip’s origin and destination, though not at a fixed, pre-determined point. Allowing passengers to walk tens—or sometimes hundreds—of meters from their starting point enables pickups and drop-offs closer to arterial roads and main avenues, contributing to more time- and cost-efficient routes. Although this option demands additional effort from passengers, it represents a middle ground between the other two alternatives.

Existing Mapped Point: In this case, the system operates with fixed pickup and drop-off points. These locations can be either physical or virtual, and if physical, they may or may not be clearly marked. This arrangement offers a certain level of predictability for both operators and passengers regarding routing. However, it may reduce accessibility for mobility-impaired groups, as they need to travel to and from these points, which could be located far from their trip origins and destinations.

Classification of Vehicle Route possible values in terms of flexibility

Free Floating Between Stops: There is no predefined route for the vehicle. Instead, the route is dynamically calculated based on current demand, adjusting to each set of trip requests. This approach prioritizes efficiency, as the vehicle only operates in areas where service has been requested. However, it requires significant computational effort for each new route calculation, and the complexity of the routing increases substantially as the number of trips grows.

Fixed Route with Diversions: The vehicle follows a predetermined fixed route but is allowed minor deviations—typically within predefined time or distance limits—to accommodate specific passenger requests. These diversions help serve passengers with special needs or reduced mobility while maintaining a degree of operational predictability for both passengers and operators. However, there is still a risk of low vehicle occupancy in areas with low demand.

Fixed Route: Vehicles follow a predetermined, unchangeable route. This operational approach offers predictability for both passengers and operators. However, it can lead to vehicles running in areas without demand, potentially lowering overall system efficiency.

Classification of Required Booking Leading Time possible values in terms of flexibility

At the Spot: No advance scheduling is required for trips; requests are fulfilled immediately, with waiting time depending on the vehicle's travel to the pick-up location. This feature offers the highest level of flexibility, as the system quickly adapts to real-time demand. It is typically applied when the DRT system aims to provide an alternative to private vehicles, offering comparable comfort and responsiveness to individual transportation.

A Prior: Trips must be requested in advance, with the required lead time varying depending on the system’s purpose. The longer the advance notice, the more time operators have to plan and optimize routes and allocate resources efficiently. However, increasing the required notice period may affect passengers’ perception of the system, as it limits the ability to accommodate spontaneous travel needs.
