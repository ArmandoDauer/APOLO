Purposes

The purposes will also serve as guideline for the decision-making process trough the entire design and operating phases of a DRT. Once these are related to the main problems the systems aim to solve, all features and decision must align with these definitions. Through the analysis of different real-world operations, we were able to narrow down the possible purposes into six classifications:

  • Reduce operational costs/Reduce pollution: The DRT system will serve as a substitute for an existing system that has been proven economically inefficient. These scenarios usually happen when there isn’t enough demand to sustain a regular service, either due to the population distribution or due to the low-demand operation hours. Also, minimize the carbon footprint of the overall transport in the area. It may be related to reduce the number of private transports in favour for the use of a public alternative (the DRT) or eliminating the inefficiencies of an existing PT that lead to excessive pollution.
  • Be a mobility alternative: In an area which relies heavily on existing public transport or private transport. Therefore, this new system will serve as an alternative transportation option. As an example, it may focus on reducing the use of private vehicles by offering a premium public transportation, with increased comfort, costing a little more than the conventional transport, but at a fraction of the cost of a private transport.
  • Serve the population in need: DRT systems ensure accessibility where traditional public transport falls short, catering to groups like seniors, people with disabilities, and those in remote areas. Their flexible, on-demand services enhance mobility options and promote community inclusivity.
  • Feed other transportation systems: The system will serve to provide access for larger transportation systems, such as airports, ports, train and bus stations. It makes a bridge between passengers’ home (or close passengers gathering locations) and these mass transportation systems.
  • Complement existing PT gaps: In these cases, the existing PT cannot cover all passenger needs. Therefore, an additional system is required to serve the demand that cannot be served by the existing service.
  • Provide transport in rural areas: DRT systems bridge gaps in rural transit by offering tailored routes and schedules. This improves access to essential services, jobs, and regional connections, reducing reliance on private vehicles and fostering sustainable community development

Up to purposes can be selected.
